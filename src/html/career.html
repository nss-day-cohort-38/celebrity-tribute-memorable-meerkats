Who the Heck is Neil Peart?

Neil Peart is the drum dude who died the other day. Now that he’s dead he can’t drum anymore, which really bums a lot of people out. When he was alive, he could drum, and people seemed to be less bummed about that. He was born in Canada, and probably said “sorry” weird. I don’t know anything about him, but I have seen his face, and not being the face of a man who apologizes, I doubt it was much of an issue.

It leaves one to wonder if he was so unapologetic on account of his character, or due to a deep-seeded insecurity about his pronunciation of the word. The likelihood of this increases when you consider that he would often be expected to express what he was sorry “aboot.”

Famous Works

Fly By Night (1975)

On the band's second album, founders Geddy Lee and Alex Lifeson welcomed new drummer (and formidable lyricist) Neil Peart into the fold. While the record wasn't a huge hit upon its release, it consolidated Rush’s impending greatness on punchy heavy rockers like "Anthem" and the title cut, as well as on widescreen epics "In the End" and the eternal "By-Tor and the Snow Dog." In many ways, Fly by Night was the group's pivotal leap forward.

2112 (1976)

The paradigm-shifting 2112 finally broke the band, even though it contradicted commercial common sense with the side-long, seven-part title suite that marries far-flung science fiction, Objectivist philosophy and mind-blowing musical progression. On the flip side, they revert to bite-sized songs of the highest order, including highlights "Something for Nothing," "Twilight Zone" and "A Passage to Bangkok."

Moving Pictures (1981)

Whether you stand by the band’s classic ‘70s period or anything that followed, there's very little dissension regarding Moving Pictures, which melds art-rock invention with mainstream appeal. "Tom Sawyer" fundamentally redefined the use of synthesizers in hard rock; "YYZ" set a new standard for rock instrumentals; "Limelight" unveiled one of Rush’s best-ever riffs; and "Red Barchetta" was, er, "Red Barchetta." Even side two’s less-celebrated numbers – the thought-provoking "Camera Eye," foreboding "Witch Hunt" and anthemic "Vital Signs" – would qualify as standouts on lesser albums. A true masterpiece of the era.
